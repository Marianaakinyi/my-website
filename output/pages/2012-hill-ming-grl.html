Citation and access

Hill, Spencer A. and Yi Ming. “Nonlinear climate response to regional brightening of tropical marine stratocumulus.” Geophysical Research Letters, 39, L15707, 5 pp. doi: 10.1029/2012GL052064. PDF. Supplementary material PDF

Hollywood-style Logline

Spraying sea salt into subtropical low-lying clouds could buy the world time to decarbonize, but things don’t add up when three different regions are seeded at once.

Summary

Exhaustively decarbonizing the world economy will be difficult, so much so that many fear we won’t be able to do so in time to avert dangerous risks of climate change. A band-aid solution, then, is “geoengineering,” i.e. the deliberate manipulation of Earth’s climate so as to counteract greenhouse gas warming. Many geoengineering methods have been suggested, such as spraying reflective sulfate particles into the stratosphere in order to (slightly) dim the sun. In this work, we investigated another, called cloud seeding.

(The term cloud seeding has two main connotations, one related to climate and the other to weather. The climate one is this geoengineering method. The weather one refers to efforts to manipulate storms by injecting them with aerosols, silver iodide being a popular option.)

Behind the scenes

This was my first ever peer-reviewed article! The work began in Summer 2010 when I worked as an intern with Yi Ming at GFDL as part of the NOAA Hollings Scholarship program. Yi had performed the simulations ahead of time, and I spent the 10 weeks while there learning how to analyze them. So on a personal level, the actual scientific content of this work, satisfied enough though I am with how it turned out, was vastly less important than the knockon effects of getting to work with Yi, as we took a liking to one another, ultimately fueling my coming back to Princeton to work with him for my PhD in Fall 2011.

In a harbinger of how my future research would tend to go (and still goes), completing the work took longer than I had allocated, and so we worked on it off and on in spurts over the next two years, before finally getting it submitted to GRL in early summer of 2012.
