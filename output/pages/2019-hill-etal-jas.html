Hill, Bordoni, and Mitchell, Axisymmetric Constraints on Cross-Equatorial Hadley Cell Extent

Since Held and Hou’s seminal 1980 paper, the theory for the time-mean Hadley cells has largely treated the cells as axisymmetric (lacking any variations in longitude), steady (lacking any variations in time), and angular momentum conserving (lacking in the free troposphere any vertical or horizontal variations in absolute angular momentum). By combining the angular-momentum-conserving (AMC) zonal wind profile with assumptions about temperature continuity and energy conservation across the cells’ expanse, Held and Hou developed a prognostic, quantitative theory for the width of the Hadley cells and a measure of its heat transport in their famous “equal-area” model.

In this paper, we attempt to draw attention to a lesser-appreciated result from that paper and those that follow, namely Hide’s Theorem as a lower bound on the /extent/ of the Hadley circulation. Hide’s theorem is well known among atmospheric dynamicists as a useful explanation for the /emergence/ of the cells (i.e. why they exist at all), but its utility as a lower bound on cell extent has largely been overlooked. Instead, those appealing to axisymmetric theory typically appeal to the aforementioned “equal-area” model (and Lindzen and Hou’s extension of it to off-equatorial forcing maxima, i.e. solsticial seasons). However, the equal-area model behaves rather poorly as the prescribed equilibrium temperature maximum is moved sufficiently far off equator, and except for in the on-equator case in the small-angle limit, it doesn’t permit of an analytical solution.

By contrast, the minimal cell extent metrics that emerge from Hide’s theorem can be computed analytically given virtually /any/ equilibrium temperature profile (since it only requires taking derivatives as opposed to integrals in the equal-area model).
