P. Brown et al 2017, “Change in the magnitude and mechanisms of global temperature variability with warming”

Both globally averaged and on a regional basis, annual-mean temperatures on Earth can vary up and down on decadal timescales purely through internal variability, i.e. even in the absence of any human influences or changes to external forcings such as the amount of the sun’s radiation reaching the planet throughout the seasonal cycle. This “decadal variability” affects human society and ecosystems throughout the globe.

At the same time, we know that humans are, in fact, altering the time-mean climate upon which the natural decadal variability acts, leading to the (forgive the pun) natural question: will decadal temperature variability itself change under global warming? In this paper, lead author Patrick Brown and co-authors Wenhong Li, Yi Ming, and myself seek to answer that question.

Using climate model simulations, we show that, compared to preindustrial conditions, decadal-timescale variability in /global-mean/ temperature weakens substantially under global warming (specifically, under quadrupled CO2). In addition, the physical processes underlying that variability change, with the decadal-scale wiggles becoming more exclusively driven by a variability mode known as the Atlantic Multidecadal Oscillation, or AMO. In contrast, /local/ decadal-scale surface temperature variability actually /increases/ over many land regions in the Tropics and subtropics, which we link in many of those locations to increased aridity: a drier surface is less able to cool via evaporation, requiring larger swings in temperature in order to regain energy balance.
